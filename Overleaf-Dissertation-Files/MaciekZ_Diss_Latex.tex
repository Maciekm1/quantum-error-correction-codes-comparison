\section{Context and Motivation}
\label{sec:intro-context}

Data security plays a vital role in modern communications by ensuring that a potential
adversary (Eve) is incapable of gaining access to sensitive information. It is theoretically
possible to secure information using encryption, where a secret key is used to transform
data into an unreadable format that can only be recovered with the same key; for this
exchange to remain secure, both the transmitter (Alice) and the receiver (Bob) must
establish a shared secret key without Eve gaining any knowledge of it
\cite{mackay_information_nodate}.

Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) provides a method for establishing these secret keys
with information-theoretic security based on quantum mechanics laws; unlike classical
cryptographic methods, secret keys are established using quantum states, and the
presence of an eavesdropper can be detected through disturbances introduced into those
states \cite{grosshans_continuous_2002}. There are two primary variants of QKD:
discrete-variable (DV) and continuous-variable (CV)
\cite{laudenbach_continuous-variable_2018}. Although DV-QKD typically requires specialized
hardware such as single-photon counters, CV-QKD utilizes standard telecommunication
components and coherent detection techniques, making it significantly easier to integrate
into existing fibre and free-space networks \cite{laudenbach_continuous-variable_2018}.

A major practical challenge for QKD is scale. Terrestrial fibre-based systems experience
distance-dependent attenuation that limits achievable range without trusted relay nodes or
quantum repeater architectures that remain challenging to deploy at scale
\cite{lauterbach_study_2023}. Independent link-budget analyses likewise suggest that
ground-only free-space quantum links are often loss-limited to the order of hundreds of
kilometres, motivating space-based range extension \cite{neumann_quantum_2017, podmore_qkd_2021}.
Satellite links, particularly in Low Earth Orbit (LEO), are therefore a strategically important
route towards global quantum-secure networking \cite{oi_cubesat_2017, lauterbach_study_2023, alvaro_space_2025}.
They can bridge intercontinental distances with only one intermediate trusted element and support
key distribution across regions that are otherwise difficult to connect \cite{podmore_qkd_2021}.

Within this context, CubeSats are attractive because they substantially reduce mission cost
and development barriers compared to conventional spacecraft \cite{oi_cubesat_2017, bouzoukis_overview_2025}.
Representative quantum-communications CubeSat concepts aim to consolidate payload and bus
hardware within envelopes on the order of $10~\mathrm{kg}$ total mass and a few litres of
volume \cite{oi_cubesat_2017}. However, CubeSats impose strict size, weight, and power (SWaP)
constraints - constraints that recur in satellite QKD payload roadmaps \cite{sivasankaran_cubesat_2022} - limited
onboard compute, finite memory, and time-varying link conditions. These constraints interact
directly with the post-processing stages of QKD, and especially with information
reconciliation (IR), where legitimate users must correct mismatches between their correlated
raw keys over an authenticated classical channel
\cite{laudenbach_continuous-variable_2018, mueller_performance_2025}.

The specific mission context for this dissertation is SPOQC (Satellite Platform for
Optical Quantum Communications), the Quantum Communications Hub's 12U CubeSat
scheduled to demonstrate in-orbit QKD from space to the Hub Optical Ground Station
(HOGS), located at the Heriot-Watt campus in Edinburgh, Scotland
\cite{noauthor_hubs_nodate, simmons_hogs_2025}. SPOQC is intended to demonstrate two different approaches to QKD using a dual quantum payload with corresponding receivers at HOGS
\cite{noauthor_hubs_nodate}.

This dissertation is motivated by a practical systems question: even when a reconciliation
algorithm is theoretically sound, is it operationally suitable for a CV-QKD CubeSat mission under realistic LEO constraints?

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:intro-problem}

Although both DV-QKD and CV-QKD are actively studied for space deployment, this
dissertation focuses on CV-QKD because it aligns with the practical engineering direction
of telecom-compatible hardware and coherent detection in constrained systems
\cite{laudenbach_continuous-variable_2018}. However, this practical advantage introduces
a demanding post-processing burden: information reconciliation in CV-QKD can become
the dominant bottleneck in low-SNR or high-noise operating regions characteristic of
long-range free-space links \cite{milicevic_quasi-cyclic_2018, gumus_novel_2021}.

The literature contains substantial work on individual reconciliation techniques, including
interactive CASCADE-style protocols and one-way LDPC-based coding approaches.
However, there is comparatively limited evidence focused on direct, application-specific
comparison between these families for LEO CubeSat CV-QKD under high-noise and
low-signal operating conditions, with explicit attention to reverse reconciliation and
mission feasibility \cite{mueller_performance_2025}. This gap is practically important
because protocol decisions at the reconciliation stage significantly affect final key
viability, classical communication overhead, and real-world deployability.

\section{Research Question}
\label{sec:research-question}

This study aims to address the following primary research question:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Under a variety of realistic practical constraints, which error correction algorithm - LDPC-based
one-way reconciliation or CASCADE-style interactive reconciliation - provides optimal performance-feasibility trade-off for CubeSat-based CV-QKD?}
\end{quote}

To make this question researchable, we also addresses the following:
\begin{itemize}
    \item How do LDPC and CASCADE compare on reconciliation efficiency and residual
    error rate across the low-to-high SNR range relevant to LEO CV-QKD?
    \item How does CASCADE's sequential round-trip interaction pattern compare to
    LDPC's one-way syndrome scheme under a realistic LEO pass-duration and RF
    classical bandwidth budget?
    \item Which algorithm is feasible on a constrained CubeSat processor, and at
    what SNR operating point?
\end{itemize}

\section{Aim and Objectives}
\label{sec:intro-aim-objectives}

The aim of this dissertation is to compare LDPC and CASCADE for reverse-reconciliation
CV-QKD in a representative LEO CubeSat scenario and determine which offers the better
performance-feasibility trade-off under realistic mission constraints.

\textbf{Objectives.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Establish the technical background required for CubeSat-based CV-QKD and
    information reconciliation.
    \item Review the state of the art on LDPC, CASCADE, and prior comparative studies,
    and identify the gap relevant to LEO CubeSat deployment.
    \item Evaluate both algorithms under matched operating conditions using a shared
    simulation and metric framework.
    \item Translate the measured results into system-level feasibility criteria covering
    communication, latency, compute, and memory.
    \item Produce a final feasibility assessment and mission-oriented recommendation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Scope, Delimitations, and Contribution}
\label{sec:intro-scope}

This dissertation is limited to the information reconciliation stage of reverse-reconciliation
CV-QKD in a LEO CubeSat context. It does not attempt a full spacecraft design, detailed
optical link modelling, or full end-to-end cryptographic integration.

Its contribution is to provide:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a focused review of reconciliation methods relevant to constrained space-based
    CV-QKD;
    \item a matched-condition comparison of LDPC and CASCADE;
    \item a first-order feasibility model linking algorithm behaviour to LEO CubeSat
    mission constraints.
\end{itemize}

\section{CubeSat Communication Systems}
\label{sec:bg-cubesat}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

CubeSats are standardised small satellites built from multiples of a
$10 \times 10 \times 10\,\text{cm}$ unit (1U) \cite{oi_cubesat_2017}. Their
main advantage is cost and accessibility: compared with conventional spacecraft,
they reduce development time and launch cost and are widely used for Earth
observation, communications, technology demonstration, and scientific missions
\cite{oi_cubesat_2017, bouzoukis_overview_2025}. This has made them an important
platform for early-stage space communications research.

Their engineering constraints are equally important. CubeSats operate within
tight size, weight, and power (SWaP) budgets, which limit payload volume,
antenna size, power availability, thermal control, shielding, onboard memory,
and processor capability \cite{oi_cubesat_2017, sivasankaran_cubesat_2022}. In
communication-intensive missions, these limits directly affect what can be
generated, stored, processed, and transmitted onboard.

LEO operation makes these constraints time-critical. A satellite in LEO has
short and intermittent contact opportunities with a fixed ground station, so
communication occurs in limited pass windows rather than continuously
\cite{oi_cubesat_2017, jordan-parra_evaluation_2024}. During a pass, link quality
changes with elevation angle, atmospheric path length, antenna geometry, and
pointing accuracy \cite{jordan-parra_evaluation_2024}. As a result, mission
performance depends not only on payload capability but on whether data handling
and communications can be completed within the available visibility window.

This is especially relevant to QKD missions. Space QKD requires both a quantum
optical link and a classical communication link, so mission viability depends on
more than optical transmission alone. Any candidate post-processing method must
fit within practical limits on pass duration, bandwidth, latency, onboard
compute, and memory. These constraints are particularly visible on 6U--12U
platforms, where a protocol that is feasible on a larger spacecraft may not
remain feasible on a CubeSat \cite{oi_cubesat_2017, simmons_hogs_2025}. For this
reason, CubeSats are not simply scaled-down satellites; they are a more
constrained operating environment in which trade-offs between algorithmic
performance and system feasibility become explicit.

\section{Information Theory}
\label{sec:bg-info-theory}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

% The communication constraints described for CubeSat missions motivate the use of
% information theory as the framework for analysing error correction, communication
% overhead, and practical reconciliation limits. 
Information theory, established by
Claude Shannon, formalises how uncertainty is quantified, how noise affects communication,
and how much information must be exchanged to recover a message reliably
\cite{mackay_information_nodate}.

A central quantity is \textit{entropy}, which measures uncertainty in a random variable.
For a binary variable with error probability $p$, the binary entropy function is
\begin{equation}
H_2(p) = -p \log_2 p - (1-p)\log_2(1-p).
\end{equation}
In reconciliation, $H_2(p)$ gives the asymptotic lower bound on the amount of
information that must be revealed to correct errors on a binary symmetric channel
\cite{mackay_information_nodate}. This is directly relevant to QKD because every
bit revealed during reconciliation is assumed to be known to an eavesdropper and
therefore reduces the final secret-key length.

Information theory also formalises the concept of a \textit{channel}: the medium over
which information is transmitted. Real channels are noisy, so received symbols may differ
from transmitted ones because of attenuation, interference, or detector noise. The
maximum reliable communication rate of such a channel is its \textit{capacity}
\cite{mackay_information_nodate}. Any practical reconciliation method must therefore
balance three competing requirements: low information leakage, reliable correction, and
implementability under finite communication and compute budgets.

In binary QKD settings, the post-sifting channel is often approximated as a binary
symmetric channel (BSC), in which each bit is independently flipped with probability $p$.
In CV-QKD, the underlying physical channel is instead continuous-valued and is more
naturally represented by a Gaussian noise model \cite{laudenbach_continuous-variable_2018}.
This distinction matters because it changes both how errors are represented and which
reconciliation techniques are suitable. In particular, CV-QKD can provide soft reliability
information that one-way codes such as LDPC can exploit, whereas interactive protocols
such as CASCADE operate only on hard binary decisions
\cite{leverrier_multidimensional_2008, milicevic_quasi-cyclic_2018}.

Within this dissertation, two broad classes of error-correction approach are relevant.
The first is \textit{syndrome-based} reconciliation, in which one party transmits a
compressed parity description derived from an error-correcting code and the other uses
that syndrome together with its own noisy data to decode. This is the basis of LDPC-based
reconciliation. The second is \textit{interactive} reconciliation, in which the parties
exchange multiple parity-based messages to locate and correct errors iteratively; CASCADE
is the standard example \cite{martinez-mateo_demystifying_2015, reis_quantum_2019}.

These two approaches represent different information-theoretic and systems trade-offs.
Syndrome-based reconciliation uses compact one-way communication but requires more local
decoding effort. Interactive reconciliation can be effective and conceptually simple, but
its cost grows with the number of messages and communication rounds
\cite{mueller_performance_2025}. In the CubeSat setting introduced in the previous
section, this distinction is critical: one-way methods are more likely to be limited by
compute, whereas interactive methods are more likely to be limited by latency and
sequential communication overhead.

\section{Quantum Key Distribution}
\label{sec:bg-qkd}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) is a method for establishing a shared secret key
between two legitimate parties, conventionally Alice and Bob, using quantum
states as information carriers. Its significance is that security can be based
on the physical properties of quantum mechanics rather than only on
computational assumptions. In particular, any attempt by an eavesdropper to
measure the transmitted quantum states introduces detectable disturbance
\cite{grosshans_continuous_2002, laudenbach_continuous-variable_2018}. QKD is
therefore attractive for long-term secure communications in settings where
classical public-key assumptions may become vulnerable.

\subsection{DV-QKD and CV-QKD}
\label{subsec:bg-dv-cv}

The two main families of QKD are discrete-variable (DV) and continuous-variable
(CV) QKD. DV-QKD encodes information in discrete quantum states, such as photon
polarisation or phase, and typically relies on single-photon detectors. These
systems have enabled many important demonstrations, but their detector
requirements can be difficult to accommodate on constrained satellite
platforms. CV-QKD instead encodes information in the continuous quadrature
amplitudes of coherent states and measures them using coherent detection, such
as homodyne or heterodyne detection. This makes CV-QKD attractive from an
engineering perspective because it uses standard telecom-compatible optical
components and avoids the detector infrastructure associated with DV-QKD
\cite{laudenbach_continuous-variable_2018}.

\subsection{GMCS CV-QKD}
\label{subsec:bg-gmcs}

This dissertation focuses on Gaussian-Modulated Coherent-State (GMCS) CV-QKD.
In GMCS protocols, Alice modulates coherent states according to a Gaussian
distribution and Bob measures them using homodyne detection
\cite{grosshans_continuous_2002, grosshans_quantum_2003}. GMCS is relevant here
because it provides a practical CV-QKD implementation based on standard
coherent laser states and homodyne detection, avoiding the need for squeezed or
entangled light. It also supports high-speed detection and multi-bit-per-pulse
Gaussian modulation, which can enable higher secret-key rates in suitable
operating regimes \cite{grosshans_quantum_2003}. For a constrained space system,
these properties make GMCS a practical engineering choice.

\subsection{The CV-QKD Process}
\label{subsec:bg-cvqkd-process}

A simplified CV-QKD pipeline consists of six stages. First, Alice prepares and
transmits Gaussian-modulated coherent states. Second, Bob performs coherent
detection and obtains a noisy continuous-valued sequence. Third, a subset of
the data is used for parameter estimation to characterise the channel and
detect suspicious excess noise. Fourth, the continuous observations are mapped
into a form suitable for binary correction, commonly using multidimensional
reconciliation \cite{leverrier_multidimensional_2008}. Fifth, an information
reconciliation protocol removes mismatches between Alice's and Bob's correlated
keys. Sixth, privacy amplification compresses the reconciled key to remove any
information revealed through noise estimation and public communication.

This structure is important because, unlike DV-QKD, CV-QKD does not produce a
binary raw key directly. The continuous-valued measurements must first be
processed into a binary representation before conventional error correction can
be applied. That extra preprocessing stage is one of the reasons reconciliation
becomes particularly demanding in CV-QKD.

\subsection{Direct and Reverse Reconciliation}
\label{subsec:bg-dr-rr}

In direct reconciliation, Bob corrects his data to match Alice; in reverse
reconciliation, Alice corrects her data to match Bob. For CV-QKD in high-loss
channels, reverse reconciliation is the practically relevant choice because it
can maintain positive key generation even when direct reconciliation fails
\cite{grosshans_continuous_2002, grosshans_quantum_2003}. This is especially
important for space-to-ground links, where free-space loss can be substantial.

In the scenario studied here, Bob corresponds to the optical ground station and
Alice to the satellite. Under reverse reconciliation, Bob measures the received
quantum states, computes the side information needed for reconciliation, and
sends that information to Alice. This fixes both the direction of the classical
communication and the location of the decoding task.

\subsection{Why Information Reconciliation Matters Here}
\label{subsec:bg-ir-role}

This dissertation focuses on the information reconciliation stage because it is
the point where information theory, CV-QKD, and CubeSat constraints intersect.
Reconciliation determines how much classical communication must be exchanged,
how much information is leaked publicly, and how much computation is required to
obtain matching keys. In a constrained LEO CubeSat mission, these factors
directly affect whether a protocol is operationally feasible \cite{mueller_performance_2025}.

\section{Summary of Background}
\label{sec:bg-summary}

This chapter has established the prerequisite knowledge needed for the rest of
the dissertation. CubeSat platforms are attractive for low-cost space

\section{Space QKD and CubeSat Context}
\label{sec:lit-space}

Satellite QKD is motivated by the distance limitations of terrestrial optical
fibre. While fibre-based QKD is effective over metropolitan and inter-city
scales, global secure communication requires methods that avoid the high
attenuation of long fibre links. Free-space optical channels therefore provide
an important route towards long-distance quantum-secure communication
\cite{lauterbach_study_2023}.

A major milestone in this area was the \textit{Micius} satellite, which
demonstrated long-distance satellite-to-ground QKD, at a distance of up to 1200km, and established the
practical viability of space-based quantum links
\cite{lauterbach_study_2023, lu_micius_2022}. It was the world's first quantum satellite mission, and although it differed significantly from a regular CubeSat platform, weighing over 600kg,it showed that quantum communication can be sustained over free-space paths far beyond the range typically achievable in fibre.

CubeSat-oriented work instead considers whether space QKD can be realised
within the mass, volume, power, and operational limits of a nanosatellite.
The CQuCoM concept makes these constraints explicit through a 6U CubeSat in
an approximately 10\,kg, 6\,L envelope, with platform power around 11\,W, a
30\,Wh battery, a 90\,mm transmit telescope, and quantum passes limited to
night-time eclipse opportunities of about 6 minutes on average
\cite{oi_cubesat_2017}. This shows that feasibility depends not only on the
optical protocol, but also on whether pointing, power storage, and pass
duration can be made compatible with quantum transmission. QEYSSat addresses
the same problem from a different systems perspective. It uses a LEO
trusted-node architecture with the satellite primarily as a receiver for
ground-to-space uplinks, shifting source complexity and much of the processing
burden to the ground segment \cite{podmore_qkd_2021}.

These studies establish the feasibility and strategic relevance of space QKD,
but they do not directly answer how a resource-constrained CubeSat should
select a reconciliation strategy when compute, memory, bandwidth, and latency
are treated as mission-level constraints.
\cite{jordan-parra_evaluation_2024}.

This issue is especially important for SPOQC, which provides a concrete
CubeSat-oriented CV-QKD mission context. Because CV-QKD requires continuous
measurement outcomes to be converted into shared binary secret keys, the
choice of reconciliation protocol becomes a central systems question. Recent
SPOQC/HOGS material indicates losses of up to around 50~dB and key rates on
the order of 100~bits/s, suggesting that mission feasibility may depend not
only on optical performance but also on the cost and behaviour of the
reconciliation stage \cite{noauthor_hubs_nodate, simmons_hogs_2025}.

\section{Information Reconciliation Approaches}
\label{sec:lit-reconciliation}

The literature on QKD reconciliation is dominated by two broad approaches:
interactive protocols such as CASCADE, and code-based protocols such as LDPC.
Both are mature and widely studied, but they embody different trade-offs in
efficiency, communication structure, and computational cost \cite{martinez-mateo_demystifying_2015}.

CASCADE remains one of the best-known reconciliation protocols in QKD. Its
appeal lies in its conceptual simplicity: errors are located through repeated
block partitioning, parity comparison, and binary search
\cite{martinez-mateo_demystifying_2015, reis_quantum_2019}. Because its
operations are explicit and message-based, CASCADE is comparatively easy to
implement and analyse. The literature shows that many optimised variants exist,
but the main point for this dissertation is that CASCADE is fundamentally a
highly interactive protocol whose communication cost and leakage depend
strongly on channel conditions and configuration choices
\cite{reis_quantum_2019, tupkary_using_2023}.

Its strengths are best established for binary-QKD channels at moderate QBER,
where it remains reasonably efficient and reliable in practice. For example,
Mart\'inez-Mateo et al.\ report reconciliation efficiencies of approximately
$f_{\mathrm{EC}} = 1.09013$ at $Q=2\%$ and $f_{\mathrm{EC}} = 1.09541$ at
$Q=5\%$, supporting the view that leakage can remain manageable in this low-noise regime
\cite{martinez-mateo_demystifying_2015}.
CASCADE, however, is highly interactive and may require many communication rounds, with
the same study showing tens to over a hundred channel uses for $10^4$-bit
frames depending on QBER and protocol configuration
\cite{martinez-mateo_demystifying_2015}. This becomes more problematic as
the SNR decreases, since improved efficiency often comes at the cost of
substantially greater interaction \cite{martinez-mateo_demystifying_2015}. 
That trade-off is particularly
relevant here because low-SNR CV-QKD, after preprocessing, may produce
effective binary channels in regimes where communication overhead and leakage
are major practical costs rather than secondary concerns.

LDPC-based reconciliation takes a different approach. Instead of repeated
two-way parity exchange, it uses one-way syndrome transmission and local
iterative decoding. This makes LDPC attractive because it can approach
Shannon-limit performance while reducing classical communication overhead. In
CV-QKD, LDPC is especially prominent because it can make use of soft
information derived from the physical channel, making it well suited to
multidimensional reconciliation and other low-SNR settings
\cite{leverrier_multidimensional_2008, milicevic_quasi-cyclic_2018}.

As with CASCADE, the literature contains many LDPC variants, including blind
and rate-adaptive methods designed to cope with uncertain or changing channel
conditions \cite{gumus_novel_2021, mueller_performance_2025}. For the present LEO CubeSat scenario,however,
all the variations share a common profile: LDPC can offer high reconciliation efficiency
with low communication overhead, but good performance often depends on code
selection, large block lengths, and sufficient computational resources for
iterative decoding \cite{milicevic_quasi-cyclic_2018, gumus_novel_2021}.

CASCADE may be easier to implement on limited hardware but can incur heavy interaction costs, while
LDPC is more communication-efficient but may demand block sizes or processing
resources that are difficult to realise on a budget-conscious CPU-only space
platform. The central literature question is therefore not which protocol is
better in the abstract, but which trade-off is more favourable under realistic
mission constraints.

\section{Comparative Literature}
\label{sec:lit-comparative}

MENTION Mueller et al consider DV-QKD and only up to 0.1 QBER

Direct comparisons between CASCADE and LDPC are less common than studies of
each protocol individually. Where they do exist, the results depend strongly
on the channel model, QKD protocol, hardware assumptions, and chosen
performance metrics.

Recent comparative work by Mueller et al shows that communication rounds, verification cost,
classical-channel behaviour, and robustness to parameter mismatch can all
change the relative attractiveness of a protocol
\cite{mueller_performance_2025}. In particular, practical studies emphasise
that protocol assessment should be application-oriented: the relevant question
is which method performs better under the constraints of the intended
deployment, not which appears stronger in isolation
\cite{mueller_performance_2025, jordan-parra_evaluation_2024}.

Another consistent lesson is that successful reconciliation does not
automatically imply useful key generation. A protocol may correct errors
correctly while still revealing too much information, or requiring too much
communication, for a useful secret key to remain after privacy amplification
\cite{mueller_performance_2025, tupkary_using_2023}. This distinction is
especially important when comparing interactive and syndrome-based methods,
because they can fail for different reasons: one through excessive leakage and
latency, the other through decoding failure or impractical resource demands.

\section{Research Gap}
\label{sec:lit-gaps}

Taken together, the literature reveals a clear gap relevant to this
dissertation. First, much comparative reconciliation work is focused on DV-QKD
or simplified binary channel models, whereas this project considers
reverse-reconciliation CV-QKD with preprocessing that preserves soft
information. Second, the low-SNR regime relevant to free-space CV-QKD is not
the regime in which CASCADE is usually most attractive. Third, existing
studies rarely translate reconciliation behaviour into a CubeSat-style mission
model with explicit limits on pass duration, bandwidth, latency, memory, and
onboard compute. Fourth, many of the strongest LDPC results assume hardware
acceleration or very large block lengths that are not obviously representative
of a CPU-only CubeSat platform
\cite{milicevic_quasi-cyclic_2018, mueller_performance_2025, jordan-parra_evaluation_2024, simmons_hogs_2025}.

This gap motivates the central approach of this dissertation: to compare
CASCADE and LDPC not only at the level of reconciliation behaviour, but also
at the level of mission feasibility within a representative LEO CubeSat model.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:lit-summary}

The literature shows that space QKD is technically viable and increasingly
oriented towards practical deployment, but that CubeSat missions introduce much
tighter systems constraints than are usually captured in protocol-level
studies. It also shows that CASCADE and LDPC represent two contrasting
reconciliation strategies: CASCADE is simple and interactive, whereas LDPC is
communication-efficient and compute-intensive. Existing comparative work makes
clear that practical evaluation must include operational constraints as well as
reconciliation efficiency. What remains insufficiently explored is the specific
combination of reverse-reconciliation CV-QKD, low-SNR operation, and CubeSat
mission feasibility. The next chapter addresses this gap through simulation-
based protocol analysis and a system-level feasibility model.

\label{cha:methodology}
% =========================================================

This dissertation uses a quantitative, simulation-based methodology to compare
LDPC and CASCADE under controlled channel conditions and then assess their
feasibility in a representative LEO CubeSat mission context. The methodology
has two parts. Part~I measures reconciliation behaviour under matched error
conditions. Part~II maps those measurements to first-order communication,
compute, and memory constraints relevant to an onboard implementation.

Simulation is appropriate because the target mission environment is not
available as a controlled testbed, the relevant operating region spans a range
of low-SNR conditions, and the two algorithms cannot be compared fairly without
systematic control of channel difficulty
\cite{mueller_performance_2025, jordan-parra_evaluation_2024}.

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\section{Part I: Simulated Performance Analysis}
\label{sec:meth-part1}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

LDPC and CASCADE operate on different internal representations. LDPC is a
one-way, soft-decision code-based scheme used here within a CV-QKD pipeline.
CASCADE is an interactive, hard-decision protocol evaluated on a binary
symmetric channel. They therefore cannot be compared on strictly identical
internal terms.

The comparison basis used in this dissertation is the measured
\textit{pre-correction BER}. On the LDPC side, SNR is swept through the full
CV-QKD simulation path and the resulting hard decisions are used to measure the
actual BER seen before decoding. On the CASCADE side, the requested BSC error
rate is adjusted so that the measured BER aligns with the LDPC operating
points. This preserves the CV-QKD interpretation on the LDPC side while
allowing cross-algorithm comparison under matched effective channel difficulty
\cite{mueller_performance_2025}.

\subsection{CV-QKD Channel Model}
\label{subsec:meth-channel}

The LDPC experiments are performed within a simplified CV-QKD simulation path
based on an additive white Gaussian noise channel:
\begin{equation}
Y = \sqrt{t}\,X + N, \qquad N \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_n^2),
\end{equation}
where $X$ is Alice's transmitted quadrature, $Y$ is Bob's received quadrature,
$t$ is effective channel transmittance, and $N$ is Gaussian noise. The
corresponding operating SNR is
\begin{equation}
\text{SNR} = \frac{t \sigma_X^2}{\sigma_n^2}.
\end{equation}

This is a fixed-SNR benchmark model rather than a full pass-level optical
simulation. In a real LEO pass, SNR varies with elevation, pointing, and
background conditions. For reconciliation benchmarking, however, a fixed
operating point is an appropriate first-order abstraction because it isolates
algorithmic behaviour from link-budget dynamics
\cite{milicevic_quasi-cyclic_2018, jordan-parra_evaluation_2024}.

\subsection{LDPC and CASCADE Pipelines}
\label{subsec:meth-pipelines}

For LDPC, each experiment follows the same sequence:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Gaussian sample generation;
    \item additive noise injection at the selected SNR;
    \item 8-dimensional multidimensional reconciliation preprocessing;
    \item hard-decision bit mapping and syndrome generation;
    \item Min-Sum LDPC decoding.
\end{enumerate}

The multidimensional reconciliation stage follows the Leverrier--Grangier 8D
procedure, which maps Gaussian-valued observations to binary variables and
produces both hard decisions and reliability information for decoding
\cite{leverrier_multidimensional_2008}.

CASCADE is evaluated separately on a binary symmetric channel. Each run applies
the standard cycle of shuffling, block partitioning, parity exchange, binary
search, and look-back correction. CASCADE is therefore not driven directly by
SNR, but by the measured BER used to align it to LDPC operating points.

\subsection{Software Implementations}
\label{subsec:meth-implementations}

LDPC is implemented using the \texttt{cvqkd-reconciliation} C library
developed for the SPOQC CV-QKD payload. Using a mission-relevant codebase makes
the measurements more representative of the intended deployment context than a
standalone pedagogical implementation. The decoder uses Min-Sum decoding on
quasi-cyclic parity-check matrices. Three matrices are evaluated:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{256x255\_z1}
    \item \texttt{512x511\_z4}
    \item \texttt{1024x1023\_z1}
\end{itemize}
These are existing low-SNR matrices available in the SPOQC codebase and were
selected as the study set for comparative testing.

CASCADE is implemented using the open-source \texttt{cascade-cpp} library
\cite{rijsman_brunorijsmancascade-cpp_2026}. This implementation records timing,
message-count, parity-request, and leakage statistics, which makes it suitable
both for direct comparison and for later latency modelling.

\subsection{Statistics Tracking Contribution}
\label{subsec:meth-tracking}

A key implementation contribution of this dissertation is the addition of a
dedicated statistics-tracking system to the internal SPOQC LDPC repository.
The original \texttt{cvqkd-reconciliation} library was extended so that each
operating point emits structured statistics in Newline-Delimited JSON
(NDJSON) format. The output schema was designed to align closely with
\texttt{cascade-cpp}, so that both algorithms could be parsed and analysed
through the same downstream workflow.

The added tracking records the main quantities required for comparison:
pre-correction bit errors and BER, post-correction bit errors and BER, frame
error rate, elapsed process time, elapsed real time, decoder iterations,
reconciliation bits, reconciliation bits per key bit, and reconciliation
efficiency.

For LDPC, these values are accumulated online over repeated independent frames
using Welford's algorithm, which provides numerically stable running means and
sample standard deviations without retaining all raw samples in memory
\cite{noauthor_algorithms_2026}. This is useful for large sweeps and high
trial counts, and it keeps the measurement layer lightweight enough to remain
compatible with the existing SPOQC implementation. The tracked output fields
for both implementations are summarised in
Appendix~\ref{chap:appendix-output}.

\subsection{Experiment Orchestration}
\label{subsec:meth-orchestration}

LDPC sweeps are orchestrated using an external Python runner driven by a YAML
configuration file. The runner varies SNR and parity-check matrix selection,
invokes the LDPC binary, and stores machine-readable outputs for later
analysis. A geometric SNR schedule is used so that the low-SNR regime is
sampled more densely, since this is the expected operating range for a LEO CubeSat and the
behaviour changes most rapidly there \cite{milicevic_quasi-cyclic_2018}. 
A representative configuration is provided
in Appendix~\ref{chap:appendix-config}.

CASCADE operating points are generated separately over requested BSC error
rates. These runs are later matched to LDPC points using the measured
pre-correction BER.

\subsection{Recorded Metrics}
\label{subsec:meth-metrics}

The comparison uses the following metrics.

\paragraph{Reconciliation efficiency.}
Efficiency is defined as
\begin{equation}
f = \frac{\text{reconciliation\_bits}}{n \cdot H_2(q)},
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the frame length and $q$ is the measured pre-correction BER.
Values close to 1 indicate operation near the Shannon limit.

\paragraph{Pre-correction BER.}
This is the measured BER before reconciliation. It is used both as a sanity
check and as the alignment variable for joint LDPC/CASCADE plots.

\paragraph{Remaining BER and FER.}
These measure post-correction reliability. For LDPC, FER is important because
frame-level decoding failure remains possible. For CASCADE, zero residual BER
in unconstrained simulation does not by itself imply mission feasibility.

\paragraph{Reconciliation bits per key bit.}
This normalises classical communication overhead across different frame sizes.

\paragraph{Elapsed process time and elapsed real time per key bit.}
These provide normalised compute-cost measures for later feasibility analysis.

\paragraph{CASCADE message count.}
This captures the number of sequential parity-message exchanges and is required
for latency modelling.

\paragraph{LDPC decoder iterations.}
This indicates decoding difficulty and its relationship to compute cost.

\subsection{Statistical Treatment}
\label{subsec:meth-statistics}

Each operating point is evaluated over repeated independent trials. For LDPC,
each trial is one independently generated frame. For CASCADE, each trial is one
full reconciliation run over a fixed binary key. Means and sample standard
deviations are computed for all primary metrics.

This statistical treatment matters because the two algorithm families exhibit
different levels of variability. LDPC frame sizes ,256, 512 and 2048 bits, in
this dissertation are short compared with the 10,000-bit CASCADE keys
used by the reference implementation, so per-frame behaviour is naturally
more variable. To improve comparability, timing and communication metrics
are normalised per key bit wherever appropriate \cite{martinez-mateo_demystifying_2015}.

\subsection{Verification}
\label{subsec:meth-verification}

Each implementation is checked against known references before being used in the
comparative study.

For CASCADE, verification consists of reproducing the expected efficiency
behaviour and comparing the resulting curves against the published
\texttt{cascade-cpp} reference graph and the established literature baseline
\cite{martinez-mateo_demystifying_2015, mueller_performance_2025}.

For LDPC, verification consists of reproducing the internal SPOQC
$1 - 2\cdot\text{BER}$ reference curve. Agreement is treated as evidence that
the Gaussian generation, multidimensional reconciliation, syndrome generation,
and Min-Sum decoding stages are behaving consistently with the original
development baseline.

A separate alignment check compares the measured pre-correction BER values used
in the matched LDPC/CASCADE plots. Any systematic mismatch here would indicate
an error in the comparison procedure rather than in the reconciliation methods
themselves. Supporting verification and alignment plots are given in
Appendix~\ref{chap:appendix-verification}.

\section{Part II: System-Level Feasibility Model}
\label{sec:meth-part2}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Mission Abstraction}
\label{subsec:meth-system-model}

The second part of the methodology translates the measured algorithmic results
into a first-order LEO CubeSat feasibility model. This is not a full mission
simulation. Its purpose is to determine whether the measured reconciliation
behaviour remains plausible under representative communication, memory, and
compute constraints.

The model assumes a Sun-synchronous orbit at approximately 500--600\,km, a
single high-quality pass per day over a fixed optical ground station, and a
120\,s quantum transmission window. Reverse reconciliation is assumed, so Bob
performs measurement and computes the classical side information at the ground
station, while Alice buffers the corresponding transmitted data onboard.

Three source-rate scenarios are considered:
\begin{itemize}
    \item 2\,MHz
    \item 10\,MHz
    \item 100\,MHz
\end{itemize}
These are used as workload cases for scaling the measured reconciliation
behaviour to mission-level throughput requirements.

Two classical-link cases are considered. In the first, a short optical
classical window is available immediately after the quantum phase. In the
second, classical communication is deferred to the next overpass and uses RF.
The purpose is not to model a final flight design, but to test whether each
reconciliation approach is structurally compatible with plausible link budgets.

\subsection{Time and Communication Model}
\label{subsec:meth-time-model}

Algorithmic outputs are interpreted through the first-order completion-time
model
\begin{equation}
T_{\text{total}} \approx T_{\text{compute}} + T_{\text{tx}} + T_{\text{latency}}.
\end{equation}

Here $T_{\text{compute}}$ is the onboard time required to perform
reconciliation on buffered data, $T_{\text{tx}}$ is the time required to
transfer the necessary classical information, and $T_{\text{latency}}$ is the
penalty due to sequential interaction.

For LDPC, the dominant terms are compute and one-way transmission. For
CASCADE, sequential message dependence makes latency a first-class constraint.
Latency is estimated as
\begin{equation}
T_{\text{latency}} = N_{\text{seq}}\tau,
\end{equation}
where $N_{\text{seq}}$ is the number of sequential message exchanges and
$\tau$ is the assumed per-round-trip latency.

\subsection{Latency Scenarios}
\label{subsec:meth-latency}

CASCADE is simulated without embedded link latency. Latency is instead applied
analytically during the feasibility stage. The following scenarios are used:
\begin{itemize}
    \item ideal simulation: $0$\,s per round-trip;
    \item optimistic RF: $0.1$\,s per round-trip;
    \item conservative RF: $0.5$\,s per round-trip;
    \item optical classical: negligible latency.
\end{itemize}
These are sensitivity-analysis cases rather than claims about a final mission
stack.

\subsection{Feasibility Criteria}
\label{subsec:meth-feasibility}

Each algorithm, operating point, and mission scenario is assessed against the
same criteria:
\begin{itemize}
    \item usable at the target SNR or matched BER;
    \item successful reconciliation, measured through remaining BER and FER;
    \item acceptable leakage, measured through efficiency and reconciliation
    bits per key bit;
    \item classical communication feasible within the assumed optical or RF
    window;
    \item sequential latency feasible within the available communication
    window;
    \item compute throughput feasible for the buffered workload;
    \item memory demand feasible for the assumed onboard buffer.
\end{itemize}

These criteria are later combined into the feasibility matrix used in the
analysis chapter.

\subsection{Scope and Limitations}
\label{subsec:meth-limitations}

Several limitations are explicit. First, the channel model uses fixed SNR
operating points rather than a dynamic pass-level optical simulation. Second,
CASCADE is compared through BER alignment rather than through a full Gaussian
CV-QKD data path. Third, all measurements are taken on development hardware,
not on flight hardware. Fourth, latency is applied analytically rather than
through a full network emulator. These choices are acceptable for a first-order
comparative study, but they limit how directly the measured timings can be
interpreted as flight performance.

\subsection{Ethical and Professional Considerations}
\label{subsec:meth-ethics}

This work raises no significant ethical concerns. It does not involve human
participants, personal data, or classified material. From a professional
standpoint, the methodology prioritises traceability and reproducibility:
configurations are stored declaratively, outputs are machine-readable, and
modelling assumptions are stated explicitly.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:meth-summary}

This chapter defined the comparative methodology used in the dissertation.
Part~I established the matched-BER comparison framework, the CV-QKD benchmark
channel, the LDPC and CASCADE experiment pipelines, the software

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The results show that algorithm choice depends on more than raw correction
performance. LDPC and CASCADE differ structurally in how they consume
computation, communication, and latency budget. The most important implication
is therefore not a single global winner, but a conditional trade-off across
operating regime and mission architecture.

Relate the final discussion explicitly to the project aim:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Which algorithm remains usable at the target low-SNR operating region?
    \item Which algorithm fits the available classical communication window?
    \item Which algorithm is compatible with CPU-only onboard processing?
    \item Which algorithm leaks less information for useful key generation?
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The analysis inherits the limitations of the methodology. The results are based
on fixed-SNR operating points, analytical latency injection, BER alignment
rather than a shared internal channel model, and measurements on development
hardware rather than flight hardware. These limitations do not invalidate the
comparative study, but they define its scope as a first-order systems analysis
rather than a final mission-performance prediction.
